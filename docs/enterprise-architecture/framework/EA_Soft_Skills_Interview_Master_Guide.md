SECTION 01

# EA Soft Skills Interview Master Guide
Panel: CEO · Board Member · CTO

Confidence, composure, credibility, and executive maturity under pressure.

###### QUESTION 1.1

### A board member publicly challenges your architecture recommendation during a steering committee. How do you respond in the moment?

###### INTERVIEW INTENT

Tests composure under executive pressure, ability to defend a position without becoming defensive, and executive maturity in high-stakes settings.

###### STRONG ANSWER INDICATORS

###### WEAK ANSWER INDICATORS

- Acknowledges the challenge without becoming defensive.

  - Becomes visibly flustered or overly apologetic.

- Separates the emotional charge from the substantive

- question.

  - Doubles down on technical arguments.

  - Defers entirely without defending their position.

- Responds with data and business framing, not technical

- detail.

  - Makes it personal.

- Invites dialogue rather than shutting it down.

- Demonstrates prior preparation for likely objections.

###### RED FLAGS

I Cannot recall a specific example of high-pressure executive engagement.

I Describes a response that would escalate rather than resolve tension.

I Shows contempt for executive questioning as 'political'.

###### FOLLOW-UP PROBES

- What if the board member was factually incorrect—how do you correct them without humiliating them?

- How do you prepare for likely objections before a major executive presentation?

- What does your body language and tone communicate in that moment?

###### ANSWER EXAMPLES — BY SENIORITY LEVEL

> **ENTERPRISE** *“I'd thank them for raising it, briefly acknowledge the concern, and redirect to the business outcome: 'That's*

> **ARCHITECT** *an important challenge—let me address the risk dimension directly.' I'd have my key data points ready and stay composed.”*

> **PRINCIPAL ARCHITECT** *“I'd use it as an opportunity to deepen the conversation. I'd restate their concern in my own words to confirm I've understood it, then provide the strongest version of my counter-argument in business language—not architecture jargon. I'd close by asking whether that addresses their concern or whether they'd like to explore it further.”*

> **DISTINGUISHED** *“Pressure in the board room is predictable. I prepare for the three most likely objections before any major*

> **ARCHITECT** *presentation, draft my responses, and rehearse them. In the moment I treat public challenge as a gift—it surfaces real concerns that would otherwise remain hidden and undermine the programme later. I stay slow, deliberate, and factual. If the challenge has merit I haven't addressed, I say so and commit to a follow-up.”*

**CTO-LEVEL**

*“Executive challenge is fundamentally about trust, not the specific objection. The board member is testing whether I can be relied upon when things get difficult. My response demonstrates exactly that: calm, evidence-based, intellectually honest. I engage with the strongest version of their argument. If they're right, I say so. If they're wrong, I show them why without making them wrong in public. The goal isn't to win the argument—it's to leave the room with their confidence strengthened, not damaged.”*

|**SCORIN**|**G RUBRIC**||
|---|---|---|
|**SCO**<br>**RE**|**LEVEL**|**CRITERIA**|
|**5**|**Distinguished**|Calm, evidence-based, invites dialogue, treats challenge as a constructive signal, clearly prepared.|
|**4**|**Strong**|Composed, good business framing, minor defensive drift, recovers quickly.|
|**3**|**Adequate**|Manages the moment but relies on technical defence or over-apologises.|
|**2**|**Weak**|Visibly rattled, becomes defensive, or defers entirely.|
|**1**|**Failing**|Escalates tension, dismisses the challenge, or cannot provide a coherent response.|

###### COACHING NOTES

Executive presence under challenge is a learnable skill. Practice delivering your key arguments in 60 seconds, in plain English, with a deliberate pace. Record yourself. The most common failure mode is speaking faster and adding more technical detail when challenged—the exact opposite of what works. Slow down, use fewer words, and end with a question that returns control to you.

###### COMMON MISTAKES

I Treating the challenge as an attack to be defended against.

I Using technical depth as a shield rather than a communication tool.

I Failing to prepare for predictable objections.

###### QUESTION 1.2

### How do you communicate deeply uncertain technical outcomes to a board that demands certainty?

###### INTERVIEW INTENT

Evaluates executive communication maturity, ability to manage expectations, and comfort with communicating uncertainty without losing credibility.

###### STRONG ANSWER INDICATORS

###### WEAK ANSWER INDICATORS

- Manufactures false precision to satisfy board expectations.

- Frames uncertainty as a risk to be managed, not a

- weakness to hide.

  - Avoids the topic of uncertainty altogether.

- Provides scenario analysis with probability-weighted

- outcomes.

  - Presents uncertainty apologetically rather than

  - professionally.

- Distinguishes what is known, what is assumed, and what is

- unknown.

- Proposes governance checkpoints to reduce uncertainty

- over time.

###### RED FLAGS

I Has never been in a situation requiring communication of uncertainty to senior leaders.

##### I Describes hiding uncertainty as a legitimate strategy

###### FOLLOW-UP PROBES

– How do you maintain your credibility when an uncertain outcome proves negative?

- What's the difference between honest uncertainty and lack of preparation?

- How do you handle a CFO who insists on a single-point ROI estimate?

###### ANSWER EXAMPLES — BY SENIORITY LEVEL

|**ENTERPRISE**<br>**ARCHITECT**|*“I'd give them a range and explain what drives the variance. I'd be clear about what we know versus what*<br>*we're assuming, and commit to specific decision points where uncertainty will reduce.”*|
|---|---|
|**PRINCIPAL ARCHITECT**|*“I'd use a three-scenario model—base, upside, downside—with explicit assumptions for each. I'd frame*<br>*uncertainty as a managed variable, not an unknown, and propose governance gates that trigger a board*<br>*conversation if we move outside the base case.”*|
|**DISTINGUISHED**<br>**ARCHITECT**|*“Boards are sophisticated enough to handle uncertainty if it's presented with rigor. I'd separate aleatory*<br>*uncertainty—inherent variability we can't eliminate—from epistemic uncertainty, which we can reduce through*|
||*action. I'd present a confidence-weighted range, identify the two or three biggest uncertainty drivers, and*<br>*show the actions we're taking to resolve each. I'd rather be accurately uncertain than precisely wrong.”*|
|**CTO-LEVEL**|*“The demand for certainty from boards is a governance design problem, not a communication problem.*|
||*Boards that expect certainty get optimistic forecasts that erode trust when reality diverges. My job is to*|
||*change that expectation systematically. I present uncertainty as a range with explicit assumptions, stage*<br>*investment gates against the resolution of key uncertainties, and create a culture where 'we don't know yet,*<br>*and here's how we'll find out' is a credible executive answer. Over time, the board learns to trust my*<br>*uncertainty estimates more than other leaders' false precision.”*|
|**SCORING RUBRIC**<br>**SCO**<br>**RE**<br>**LEVEL**|**CRITERIA**|
|**5**<br>**Distinguished**|Scenario framing, assumption transparency, governance gates, reshapes board expectations.|
|**4**<br>**Strong**|Range-based, assumption-driven, minor gaps in governance design.|
|**3**<br>**Adequate**|Acknowledges uncertainty but lacks structured framework or governance linkage.|
|**2**<br>**Weak**|Manufactures precision or treats uncertainty as something to hide.|
|**1**<br>**Failing**|Cannot articulate a principled approach to communicating uncertainty.|

###### COACHING NOTES

The credibility trap is real: leaders who communicate false certainty get short-term approval but long-term erosion of trust. Build your comfort with uncertainty language. Phrases like 'our current best estimate is X, with the primary drivers of variance being Y and Z' are executive-grade communication. Practice them until they feel natural.

###### COMMON MISTAKES

I Confusing precision with accuracy.

I Treating uncertainty as a sign of inadequate preparation.

I Not providing a structured path to reduce uncertainty.

SECTION 02

# EXECUTIVE COMMUNICATION

Panel: CFO · CEO · Board Member

Storytelling, business language, strategic framing, and the ability to make complexity clear.

###### QUESTION 2.1

**Explain the strategic risk of technical debt to a CFO who believes it is an engineering problem, not a finance problem.**

###### INTERVIEW INTENT

Tests business communication skill, ability to translate technical concepts into financial language, and executive persuasion without jargon.

###### STRONG ANSWER INDICATORS

###### WEAK ANSWER INDICATORS

- Uses financial analogies: interest, carrying cost, compound

- liability.

  - Explains technical debt in architectural terms.

  - Uses engineering metrics (story points, sprint velocity)

  - without translation.

- Quantifies the business impact: velocity reduction, incident

- cost, talent cost.

  - Cannot quantify business impact.

- Links technical debt to revenue risk, not just engineering

- inconvenience.

  - Acknowledges it as an engineering problem.

- Proposes a remediation model with staged financial

- commitment.

###### RED FLAGS

I Cannot articulate a single financial consequence of technical debt.

- I Frames the issue as the CFO not understanding technology.

###### FOLLOW-UP PROBES

- How do you prioritise which technical debt to address first?

- How do you measure the ROI of technical debt remediation?

- What does unmanaged technical debt eventually look like to a board?

###### ANSWER EXAMPLES — BY SENIORITY LEVEL

**ENTERPRISE ARCHITECT**

*“Technical debt is like carrying high-interest revolving credit. Every feature we build on top of it costs more than it should, and the interest compounds. I'd show the CFO the trend line: our delivery cost per feature has grown 35% in three years because of the debt we're carrying.”*

**PRINCIPAL ARCHITECT**

*“I'd reframe it as a balance sheet item. Technical debt is deferred investment that accumulates a carrying cost—slower delivery, higher incident rates, and increased talent attrition because engineers don't want to work in degraded systems. I'd quantify each: our current debt is estimated to cost £4M per year in lost delivery capacity and £1.2M in additional incident management. The remediation investment is £6M over two years with a payback period of 18 months.”*

|**DISTINGUISHED**<br>|*“The CFO analogy that lands best is deferred maintenance on a factory floor. If you don't maintain the*|
|---|---|
|**ARCHITECT**|*machinery, output degrades gradually until something breaks catastrophically. Technical debt has the same*|
||*profile: it accumulates silently, creates hidden carrying costs, and eventually triggers an unplanned event—a*|
||*major outage, a security breach, a regulatory failure—that costs far more to remediate under pressure than it*|
||*would have cost to manage proactively. I'd bring the data on comparable incidents in our sector and the*<br>*regulatory exposure our current architecture creates.”*|
|**CTO-LEVEL**|*“I'd change the framing entirely. Technical debt is not an engineering problem—it is an accumulated*|
||*consequence of business decisions made under time pressure over years. Every time we asked engineering*|
||*to ship faster than the architecture could support, we borrowed capacity from the future. The CFO approved*|
||*those decisions. My job now is to make the liability visible on the balance sheet and propose a structured*|
||*programme to convert it to an asset. I'd present three scenarios: continue as-is (cost trajectory and incident*|
||*probability), selective remediation (highest-ROI debt first), and systematic modernisation. The CFO gets to*<br>*decide the risk appetite.”*|
|**SCORING RUBRIC**||
|**SCO**<br>**RE**<br>**LEVEL**|**CRITERIA**|
|**5**<br>**Distinguished**|Financial analogy, quantified impact, balance sheet framing, business decision linkage, CFO-grade language.|
|**4**<br>**Strong**|Good financial framing, some quantification, minor technical language drift.|
|**3**<br>**Adequate**|Attempts business framing but cannot fully escape technical vocabulary.|
|**2**<br>**Weak**|Engineering-centric explanation with minimal financial translation.|
|**1**<br>**Failing**|Cannot articulate financial impact; frames as an engineering problem.|

###### COACHING NOTES

Build a library of financial analogies for common architectural concepts. Technical debt = deferred maintenance or compound interest. Architecture = city planning. Platform = factory floor. Microservices = franchise model. When you can switch fluently between the technical reality and the financial metaphor, you can hold a conversation with a CFO without translation friction.

###### COMMON MISTAKES

I Expecting the CFO to translate from engineering to finance.

I Using velocity or sprint metrics without a financial denominator.

I Not quantifying the business impact before the conversation.

SECTION 03

# INFLUENCE WITHOUT AUTHORITY

Panel: Chief Enterprise Architect · VP Engineering · Distinguished Engineer

Persuasion, coalition building, trust development, and long-term influence without a mandate.

###### QUESTION 3.1

**Product leadership has committed to a delivery timeline that requires bypassing your architecture standards. How do you respond?**

###### INTERVIEW INTENT

Tests influence skill, ability to hold a position under pressure, and political sophistication in managing competing executive agendas.

###### STRONG ANSWER INDICATORS

###### WEAK ANSWER INDICATORS

- Does not rely on authority or process to block delivery.

  - Invokes architecture governance as a veto.

- Quantifies the risk of the bypass in terms the CPO cares

- about.

  - Escalates to the CTO immediately.

  - Accepts the bypass without documenting the risk.

- Proposes an alternative that meets the timeline with

- managed risk.

  - Argues on principle rather than business merit.

- Builds a relationship with the CPO rather than treating

- them as an adversary.

- Creates a documented risk-acceptance path if the bypass

- proceeds.

###### RED FLAGS

I Has no toolkit for influencing decisions already made by peers.

I Treats all architecture standards as equally non-negotiable.

- I Cannot distinguish between a standards deviation and a genuine risk.

###### FOLLOW-UP PROBES

- How do you prevent this situation from recurring in the next programme?

- What if the CPO insists the architecture team is the bottleneck?

- How do you maintain a productive relationship with the CPO after this conflict?

###### ANSWER EXAMPLES — BY SENIORITY LEVEL

> **ENTERPRISE** *“I'd ask for 30 minutes with the CPO to walk through the specific risks of the bypass—framed as 'I want to*

> **ARCHITECT** *make sure you have what you need to make this call with full information.' I'd offer a modified approach that addresses 80% of the standard with minimal timeline impact.”*

**PRINCIPAL ARCHITECT**

*“I'd start by understanding the business driver for the timeline—there's usually a specific commercial event or commitment. If I understand that, I can find the minimum deviation from standards that enables delivery. I'd propose a conditional exception: we bypass this standard now, with a documented remediation item in the next sprint and a shared commitment from the product team. I'd make the risk visible and put the accountability where it belongs—with the business decision maker.”*

> **DISTINGUISHED** *“My influence strategy is built before these situations arise. I invest in the CPO relationship continuously so*

> **ARCHITECT** *that when I say 'this matters,' they believe me. In this specific situation, I'd reframe the architecture standard as a product risk, not an architectural principle. What is the customer, commercial, or regulatory exposure if this fails in production? I'd quantify it, propose the lowest-friction compliant path, and offer to personally accelerate the architecture review. If the bypass proceeds anyway, I'd formally document the decision and the risk acceptance with the CPO's signature—not as a gotcha, but as shared governance.”*

|**CTO-LE**|**VEL**|*“This is a governance design failure that I'd address structurally. Architecture standards that can be bypassed*|
|---|---|---|
|||*without consequence have no real effect. I'd redesign the operating model: standards are tiered by risk, the*|
|||*high-risk tier has an automated enforcement mechanism, and exceptions require a formal risk acceptance*<br>*signed by the business owner. In the immediate situation, I'd engage the CPO directly: 'I'm not blocking your*<br>*delivery—I'm protecting your delivery from a production failure that would cost you the timeline gains three*<br>*months from now.' That reframe usually changes the conversation.”*|
|**SCORIN**|**G RUBRIC**||
|**SCO**<br>**RE**|**LEVEL**|**CRITERIA**|
|**5**|**Distinguished**|Risk reframe, relationship investment, tiered standards, formal risk-acceptance path, structural prevention.|
|**4**|**Strong**|Good influence approach, business framing, conditional exception, minor structural gaps.|
|**3**|**Adequate**|Attempts negotiation but over-relies on process or authority.|
|**2**|**Weak**|Blocks delivery through governance or accepts bypass without documentation.|
|**1**|**Failing**|No influence strategy; either capitulates or escalates.|

###### COACHING NOTES

The key mental shift is from 'my job is to enforce standards' to 'my job is to protect the business from the risks that standards address.' When you operate from the second framing, product leaders engage with you differently. Build the CPO relationship in calm periods so you have capital to spend when tensions arise.

###### COMMON MISTAKES

I Treating architecture governance as a veto power rather than a risk management tool.

I Not having pre-existing relationships with product leadership before conflicts arise.

I Failing to distinguish between high-risk and low-risk standards deviations.

SECTION 05

# STRATEGIC THINKING

Panel: CEO · Board Member · Chief Transformation Officer

Long-horizon thinking, systems reasoning, and the ability to align technology to shifting business strategy.

###### QUESTION 5.1

**Your company is considering a major acquisition. What is your role as Chief Architect and what are the first three things you do?**

###### INTERVIEW INTENT

Evaluates strategic instinct, ability to operate in ambiguous high-stakes situations, and understanding of architecture's role in M&A.;

###### STRONG ANSWER INDICATORS

###### WEAK ANSWER INDICATORS

- Immediately focuses on architecture compatibility, not

- technology inventory.

  - Focuses on infrastructure inventory.

  - Treats M&A; as a technology project rather than a business

  - transformation.

- Distinguishes pre-close and post-close responsibilities.

  - No mention of due diligence framework or integration risk.

- Engages with business integration goals, not just IT

- integration.

- Raises data sovereignty, security, and regulatory

- considerations early.

- Creates a structured due diligence framework rather than

- reacting ad hoc.

###### RED FLAGS

I Has never been involved in M&A; architecture work.

- I Cannot articulate the difference between technology integration and business integration.

###### FOLLOW-UP PROBES

- How do you assess whether the target company's architecture will accelerate or impede the acquisition rationale?

- What governance model do you establish for the integration period?

- How do you manage the people risk in the target company's architecture team?

###### ANSWER EXAMPLES — BY SENIORITY LEVEL

> **ENTERPRISE** *“First, I'd get access to the architecture due diligence data room. I'd map the target's major platforms against*

> **ARCHITECT** *our own for compatibility and conflict. Second, I'd assess integration complexity—not just technical but organisational. Third, I'd produce a risk-tiered integration roadmap with the top five architecture risks clearly labelled.”*

> **PRINCIPAL ARCHITECT** *“My first action is to understand the acquisition rationale in business terms—are we buying a customer base, a technology capability, or a market position? That defines the integration architecture. Then I'd build a three-lens due diligence: technical estate (debt, scalability, security posture), data architecture (sovereignty, quality, integration complexity), and organisational architecture (team structure, decision-making, culture alignment). The third lens is the most underestimated.”*

**DISTINGUISHED ARCHITECT**

*“M&A; architecture work is about acquisition thesis protection. If the deal thesis is 'we're buying their customer data platform,' then I focus exclusively on whether that platform can actually deliver what the thesis assumes—at scale, in our regulatory environment, integrated with our commercial systems. Everything else is secondary. I've seen acquisitions fail not because of technology incompatibility but because the acquiring company couldn't absorb the target's engineering culture. Architecture due diligence has to surface organisational risks as prominently as technical ones.”*

|**CTO-LE**|**VEL**|*“I'm involved in M&A; strategy before a target is identified, not after. My role is to define what kind of*|
|---|---|---|
|||*acquisition would accelerate our strategy—what capabilities we lack and could not build faster than we could*|
|||*acquire. When a specific target is identified, I run a parallel due diligence track to the financial one:*|
|||*architecture compatibility, data estate quality, regulatory exposure, and organisational design. I produce a*|
|||*single-page integration risk assessment for the board before the deal closes. The most important question I*|
|||*answer is: 'Does this acquisition add strategic capability or strategic complexity?' Often it's both—the board*|
|||*needs to know which dominates.”*|
|**SCORIN**|**G RUBRIC**||
|**SCO**<br>**RE**|**LEVEL**|**CRITERIA**|
|**5**|**Distinguished**|Thesis-driven, organisational risk included, pre-close involvement, board-level framing.|
|**4**|**Strong**|Good due diligence structure, business integration focus, minor gaps in pre-close strategy.|
|**3**|**Adequate**|Reasonable approach but too technology-centric; misses organisational dimension.|
|**2**|**Weak**|Infrastructure inventory focus; no acquisition thesis linkage.|
|**1**|**Failing**|Cannot articulate an M&A; architecture due diligence framework.|

###### COACHING NOTES

If you haven't worked on an M&A; deal, build the framework theoretically and then find a mentor who has. The most important thing to communicate is that you understand M&A; as a business transformation event with an architecture dimension—not an IT project. Interviewers at the Chief Architect level will probe this heavily.

###### COMMON MISTAKES

I Treating M&A; architecture as infrastructure inventory.

I Not engaging with the acquisition rationale before designing the integration approach.

I Underestimating the organisational and cultural architecture dimensions.

SECTION 08

# CHANGE LEADERSHIP

Panel: Chief Transformation Officer · COO · CEO

Transformation experience, change management capability, and leadership maturity.

###### QUESTION 8.1

**You are leading a cloud transformation that has strong executive sponsorship but persistent engineering resistance. Eighteen months in, morale is low and adoption is behind target. What do you do?**

###### INTERVIEW INTENT

Tests transformation resilience, diagnostic ability, and leadership maturity under sustained pressure.

###### STRONG ANSWER INDICATORS

###### WEAK ANSWER INDICATORS

- Diagnoses before prescribing: understands why resistance

- persists.

  - Doubles down on communication and change

  - management activity.

- Distinguishes between rational resistance (legitimate

- concerns) and emotional resistance (fear of change).

  - Escalates engineering resistance to executive sponsors as

  - a performance issue.

  - Attributes failure to team attitude rather than programme

  - design.

- Does not blame the engineering teams.

- Revisits the transformation design, not just the

- communication plan.

- Considers whether the programme should be redesigned,

- paused, or stopped.

###### RED FLAGS

I Has never led a transformation through a trough period.

I Cannot distinguish between a recoverable programme and one that should stop.

I No self-reflection on what the programme design got wrong.

###### FOLLOW-UP PROBES

- At what point do you recommend stopping a transformation rather than continuing?

- How do you manage your own morale and leadership presence when a programme is struggling?

- What would you do differently if you were designing this programme from scratch today?

###### ANSWER EXAMPLES — BY SENIORITY LEVEL

> **ENTERPRISE** *“I'd run a structured retrospective with the engineering leads—not to assign blame but to understand the real*

> **ARCHITECT** *blockers. Are they technical, organisational, or skills-based? Then I'd redesign the approach based on what I find and be transparent with the sponsors about what needs to change.”*

> **PRINCIPAL ARCHITECT** *“Eighteen months of persistent resistance usually means the programme design has a structural flaw. I'd stop the current approach and run a diagnostic: interviews with engineering leads, product owners, and a sample of individual contributors. I'd look for the gap between the executive narrative ('this is good for everyone') and the engineering reality ('this is adding to our workload without reducing it'). Then I'd redesign the programme around closing that gap—starting with quick wins that deliver real benefit to the engineering teams, not just to the business.”*

|**DISTINGUISHED**<br>|*“Persistent resistance despite executive sponsorship is a classic second-order transformation problem. The*|
|---|---|
|**ARCHITECT**|*sponsorship addresses the organisational permission to change; it doesn't address the individual motivation*|
||*to change. I'd audit the WIIFM—'What's in it for me?'—for every affected engineering team. If there's no*|
||*compelling answer, no amount of executive pressure will move the needle. I'd also examine the incentive*|
||*structure: are engineering leads measured on outcomes that reward the target state, or on delivery metrics*<br>*that the transformation temporarily degrades? You cannot transform what you reward against.”*|
|**CTO-LEVEL**|*“A transformation in distress after 18 months has usually failed at the programme design level, not the*|
||*execution level. I'd convene an honest reset: bring in an independent perspective (internal or external), run a*|
||*structured review of the original case for change against current reality, and produce a 'continue, pivot, or*|
||*stop' recommendation for the board within 30 days. The most destructive thing is to continue a programme*<br>*everyone knows isn't working because nobody wants to be the one who says so. Leadership credibility is built*<br>*by calling it early, not by engineering a late save.”*|
|**SCORING RUBRIC**||
|**SCO**<br>**RE**<br>**LEVEL**|**CRITERIA**|
|**5**<br>**Distinguished**|Structural diagnosis, WIIFM analysis, incentive audit, honest reset recommendation, self-reflection.|
|**4**<br>**Strong**|Good diagnostic approach, redesign instinct, minor gap in self-reflection.|
|**3**<br>**Adequate**|Recognises design flaw but defaults to communication and change management solutions.|
|**2**<br>**Weak**|Doubles down on execution without questioning the programme design.|
|**1**<br>**Failing**|Blames engineering teams; cannot diagnose structural failure.|

###### COACHING NOTES

The hardest leadership skill in transformation is the willingness to declare that the programme design is wrong and needs to change. Most leaders treat the programme plan as a commitment rather than a hypothesis. Practise framing transformation as iterative: the design is a best current hypothesis, and evidence of persistent resistance is data that should update the hypothesis.

###### COMMON MISTAKES

I Treating persistent resistance as a communication problem.

I Not auditing the incentive structures that reward the old behaviour.

I Failing to involve engineering teams in redesigning the approach.

SECTION 11

# EMOTIONAL INTELLIGENCE

Panel: Executive Leadership Coach · Organisational Psychologist

Self-awareness, empathy, emotional regulation, resilience, and the ability to manage difficult interpersonal dynamics.

###### QUESTION 11.1

**A senior engineer you deeply respect publicly disagrees with your architectural decision in a design review, in front of junior engineers. How do you handle it?**

###### INTERVIEW INTENT

Tests emotional regulation, psychological safety leadership, and ability to model the intellectual humility expected of senior architects.

###### STRONG ANSWER INDICATORS

###### WEAK ANSWER INDICATORS

- Defers discussion to a private meeting to avoid the topic in

- front of juniors.

- Treats the disagreement as a constructive contribution, not

- a challenge to authority.

- Engages with the substance of the objection in the room.

  - Uses seniority to close down the debate.

  - Becomes visibly uncomfortable.

- Does not reassert seniority as the answer.

- Models intellectual humility for the junior engineers

- watching.

  - Accepts the objection without engaging with it.

- If the engineer has a point, says so explicitly.

###### RED FLAGS
