# Transcript: Healthcare Network — Executive Steering Committee (Month 6 Update)

**Engagement:** Ambient AI Clinical Documentation Programme
**Session:** Executive Steering Committee — Month 6 Programme Update
**Duration:** 45-minute slot (09:00 – 09:45)
**Location:** Board Room, Network Head Office

**Programme Lead:**
- Rachel Osei — EA Lead, Digital Health Transformation

**Executive Steering Committee:**
- Dr. Patricia Lim — Chief Executive Officer
- David Torres — Chief Financial Officer
- Dr. Ahmad Karimi — Chief Medical Officer
- Lena Park — Chief Information Officer

---

*Rachel has one slide deck open on the room screen — 12 slides. She has distributed a one-page summary to each attendee before sitting down. The room is configured boardroom-style; four executives on one side of the table, Rachel at the head. The session is running exactly on time.*

---

**Rachel Osei:** Good morning. I want to get straight into it because we have 45 minutes and I want to leave the last 15 for your questions and a decision. I'm going to open with the number that matters most, because it's not the number we hoped to report at month six.

Physician adoption of the ambient documentation system is 61%. Our target for month six was 80%.

> **Facilitator Note:** Rachel has opened by disclosing the adoption gap in her first 30 seconds. This is a deliberate choice, and it is the right one. The alternative — leading with positive metrics and surfacing the adoption shortfall 20 minutes in, or in an appendix slide — guarantees a worse conversation. David Torres's team will have seen the adoption data independently; if Rachel had buried it, the CFO would have named it himself, in a context where Rachel looked like she had tried to hide a problem. By disclosing it first, Rachel converts the narrative from "the programme has a problem we didn't hear about until now" to "the programme lead flagged a problem and came with a plan." The first narrative destroys credibility. The second builds it.

**David Torres:** *(looking down at the one-page summary)* 61 versus 80. That's a 24% miss on a KPI that drives most of your projected benefit case.

**Rachel Osei:** That's correct. I want to walk through why that gap exists, what it means for the programme benefits, and what we're doing about it. I'll start with the why.

In the pilot phase — which ran at Southgate Clinic and City North Hospital — adoption reached 78%. We extrapolated that figure to the full network rollout. What we underweighted was the difference between early adopter physician cohorts and the broader population. Our pilot sites had higher-than-average proportions of physicians who volunteered to participate. The network-wide rollout included mandatory participation for all physicians in the participating departments, and the cohort characteristics are different — older average tenure, higher proportion of physicians who were not involved in the original system selection, and a UI feedback pattern that we hadn't seen in the pilot: physicians are turning off the ambient capture after the first 15 minutes of a consultation because the visual transcription overlay is distracting during complex patient interactions.

**Dr. Ahmad Karimi:** That last point — the overlay distraction — we've been hearing this in the physician forums. It's not universal. In primary care it's less of an issue. In oncology and complex chronic disease management it's a significant complaint.

**Rachel Osei:** Agreed. The speciality segmentation is in slide four. The adoption gap is concentrated in oncology, complex chronic disease, and inpatient surgery. Primary care is at 74% — still below target, but much closer.

> **Facilitator Note:** Rachel is not being defensive about the gap. She is offering a precise, segmented diagnosis — which physicians, in which specialities, for which specific reasons. This is what separates a professional bad-news delivery from an unprofessional one. Vague explanations ("the rollout was harder than expected") invite scepticism and invite executives to fill the vacuum with their own, often harsher, interpretations. Specific explanations invite engagement with the diagnosis. David Torres is already holding the one-pager rather than shutting down — that is the signal that the specificity is working.

---

**David Torres:** Let me ask the financial question directly. The benefit case projected $18M in annualised physician time savings based on 35 minutes per physician per day recaptured from documentation. If adoption is 61%, and I assume the adopting physicians are getting proportionate benefit, I'm getting to roughly $11M annualised. That's a $7M shortfall against the benefit case. Is that your math too?

**Rachel Osei:** Your calculation is directionally right, but there's a nuance worth noting. The time savings data we have from the active adopters is slightly above the projection — we're seeing 38 minutes per day on average rather than 35, from physicians who are using the system consistently. So the per-adopter benefit is outperforming. The shortfall is volume, not per-unit benefit.

**David Torres:** That helps, but it doesn't change the bottom line much. The question I haven't gotten an answer to yet: are these time savings showing up in overtime reduction? Because 38 minutes per physician per day is a real number if it's reducing paid overtime. It's a theoretical number if physicians are using the recaptured time for other unbillable activities.

> **Facilitator Note:** This is the sharpest financial question in the session, and possibly the hardest. David is asking whether the benefit has materialised in a measurable operational line — specifically, overtime. This is a classic CFO move: translating a programme metric (time saved per physician) into a P&L line (overtime cost). If Rachel cannot answer this, it implies the benefits tracking has been incomplete. If she can answer it, even partially, it demonstrates that the programme team is managing benefits realisation seriously, not just reporting vanity metrics.

**Rachel Osei:** We have partial data on that. Slide six. Among the 14 departments with full adoption data at the month six mark, eight have reported measurable reductions in after-hours documentation time — which is the primary driver of overtime claims for this cohort. The average overtime cost reduction in those eight departments is $8,400 per month per department, which extrapolates to approximately $1.4M annualised across those departments alone.

The departments with lower adoption — predominantly the oncology and complex care specialities I mentioned — are not yet showing overtime reduction because the adoption rate in those departments is below the threshold where we'd expect systemic schedule changes. We don't expect to see it there until adoption clears 70%.

**David Torres:** So $1.4M annualised from eight departments. What's the total number of departments in scope?

**Rachel Osei:** 34.

**David Torres:** So we have confirmed overtime benefit from 24% of the in-scope departments, and the rest is projection.

**Rachel Osei:** Yes. That's accurate.

**David Torres:** Thank you for being straight about that.

> **Facilitator Note:** David's acknowledgement — "thank you for being straight about that" — is diagnostic. A CFO who says this has been given enough prior experience of programme leads being not-straight that the straight answer registers as notable. Rachel's willingness to confirm the partial nature of the overtime data, rather than pivoting to a more favourable metric, has earned a CFO's explicit trust. That trust is worth more in the next difficult conversation than any individual benefit claim.

---

**Dr. Ahmad Karimi:** I want to add some context to the adoption number before we go further, because 61% doesn't capture what I'm seeing in physician sentiment.

We ran an NPS survey across participating physicians at month two and again at month five. At month two, NPS was 23 — net negative, with physicians citing the learning curve and a perception that the system was adding steps. By month five, NPS had moved to 54. That's a 31-point improvement in three months, and it correlates with the physicians who moved from initial use to consistent use. The physicians who are below adoption threshold are disproportionately those who tried it once, had a friction experience, and stopped. The ones who got through the friction curve are advocates.

**Dr. Patricia Lim:** That's a meaningful signal, Ahmad. Rachel, how are you using that?

**Rachel Osei:** It's the core logic behind the corrective action I'm about to propose. The NPS trajectory tells us the product works — the physicians who use it consistently become advocates. The challenge is getting the resisters through the initial friction. The solution is not a better product; it's a better onboarding pathway.

---

**Dr. Patricia Lim:** Before we get to corrective action — I want to ask the question you've probably been expecting. Should we continue this programme?

> **Facilitator Note:** Patricia is asking the governing question directly. This is appropriate for a CEO and it is also, in a subtle way, a trust test. She is giving Rachel the space to answer honestly, including the answer "maybe not." A CEO who asks this question is offering the programme lead an exit ramp if the programme lead believes one is warranted. Rachel's response to this question will determine whether the committee treats her as a credible independent advisor or as an advocate for her own programme.

**Rachel Osei:** My recommendation is to continue, and I want to be transparent about how I arrived at that recommendation.

The case for pausing or stopping: adoption is below target, the full benefit case has not materialised, and there is a cohort of physicians who have tried the system and disengaged. If those physicians' disengagement is permanent, the programme cannot reach its targets without a material product change or a different implementation approach.

The case for continuing: the NPS trajectory suggests the disengagement is addressable. The overtime benefit is real in the departments that have crossed the adoption threshold. The per-adopter benefit is outperforming the projection. And the corrective action I'm proposing is targeted and low-cost relative to the remaining programme investment.

My honest assessment is that stopping now would crystallise a partial benefit that does not justify the investment to date. Continuing with the corrective action I'm about to describe has a high probability of closing the adoption gap and delivering the full benefit case within the next four months.

If the corrective action does not produce measurable improvement by month eight, I will recommend at that point that we pause and reassess. That's a commitment I'm making to this committee now.

**Dr. Patricia Lim:** Good. Let's hear the corrective action.

> **Facilitator Note:** Rachel's answer to the governing question is structured precisely: she explicitly presents the case against continuing before presenting her recommendation to continue. This structure matters enormously. A programme lead who only makes the case for their programme is not credible. A programme lead who makes both cases and then states a reasoned recommendation is behaving as an independent advisor. Patricia's "good" is not casual — it signals that the structure of the answer demonstrated the integrity she was testing for.

---

**Rachel Osei:** Two corrective actions, both executable in the next six weeks.

First: a physician champion programme. We will identify eight to ten physicians who are at or above the 70% usage threshold and who have a positive NPS — we have these names from the month five survey. We'll ask them to serve as peer mentors for a structured six-week cohort programme targeting the disengaged physicians. The evidence on peer-led adoption in clinical technology is strong — a physician listening to another physician's workflow story is substantially more effective than any IT onboarding session or vendor training. Estimated cost: 0.2 FTE of programme management time, no external spend required.

Second: a targeted UI simplification for the overlay. The transcription overlay in complex consultations is a specific, addressable friction. Our implementation vendor has confirmed that the overlay can be configured to minimise by default and surface only on physician command, with a 3-second re-activation. This removes the distraction complaint without removing the functionality. Estimated effort: two weeks of vendor configuration, no additional licence cost.

**Lena Park:** The vendor change — does that need a change request process? I want to make sure we're not touching production configuration without proper review.

**Rachel Osei:** Good question. The configuration change falls within our existing change management protocol — it's a settings-level change, not a code change. I've already confirmed with your team, Lena, that it can go through the standard monthly change window. We're targeting the August 12th window.

**Lena Park:** Good. I'd want to see the change documented through the normal process.

**Rachel Osei:** Already in draft — I can share the change request record with you after this session.

> **Facilitator Note:** Rachel's answer to Lena's process question demonstrates pre-meeting preparation. She has already confirmed the change management pathway with Lena's team before the steering committee. This is not just efficiency — it removes a potential objection before it becomes a reason to delay. The worst version of this moment is Rachel saying "I'll check with your team" — which introduces uncertainty and implies she hasn't thought through the operational pathway. Preparedness on process details signals to committee members that the programme team is not just strategically competent but operationally disciplined.

---

**David Torres:** If the physician champion programme and UI fix close the gap and get you to 80% by month eight — what does the revised benefit case look like?

**Rachel Osei:** The revised benefit case with an 80% adoption target achieved at month eight rather than month six: full-year benefit is $16.2M versus the original $18M projection. The $1.8M reduction reflects the two-month adoption delay. It does not affect the payback period materially — we're still inside 14 months on total programme cost.

**David Torres:** And if you don't get to 80%? What does 70% look like?

**Rachel Osei:** At 70% sustained adoption, annualised benefit is $13.1M. Payback extends to 17 months. That's still inside our internal hurdle rate for this programme category, which is 24 months.

**David Torres:** I appreciate the sensitivity scenario. Most programme updates I get don't include the downside case.

> **Facilitator Note:** The sensitivity scenario — what happens at 70%? — was not in Rachel's prepared slides. She answered it immediately with specific numbers. This means she had modelled the downside before the meeting, anticipating the question. Anticipating CFO questions is a basic professional discipline in investment committee contexts; the fact that David explicitly comments on it suggests it is not universal. Building sensitivity tables for benefit cases is the EA practitioner's equivalent of a lawyer preparing the counterargument: you should always know the downside numbers before you walk into the room.

---

**Dr. Patricia Lim:** Let's go to decision. I'm going to ask for a show of hands, but before I do — does anyone want to put anything else on the table?

*(Silence.)*

**Dr. Patricia Lim:** The recommendation on the table is to continue the programme, execute the physician champion programme and UI overlay fix over the next six weeks, and reassess at month eight with a specific decision gate. Are we agreed?

**David Torres:** Agreed. I want the month eight decision gate formally documented in the programme charter — not just a verbal commitment.

**Rachel Osei:** Understood. I'll issue a charter amendment by end of this week.

**Dr. Ahmad Karimi:** Agreed. The physician champion selection — I'd like to be consulted on the names. Some of the physicians in that cohort report to my office and I want to make sure the asks are coordinated.

**Rachel Osei:** Of course. I'll send you the proposed list before we approach anyone.

**Lena Park:** Agreed.

**Dr. Patricia Lim:** Agreed. Rachel, this was a well-run update. The committee has made its decision: continue with corrective action, month eight gate. Thank you.

> **Facilitator Note:** Patricia's closing comment — "this was a well-run update" — is a meaningful signal from a CEO who has seen many poorly run updates. What made it well-run was not the quality of the results (which were below target). It was the structure: bad news surfaced first, specific diagnosis, financial questions answered honestly and quantitatively, governing question addressed with both the pro and con arguments, corrective action proposed and operationally grounded, downside scenarios modelled. The meeting ended two minutes early and produced a clear decision. These are not accidents.

## Post-Session Reflection: What Made This Presentation Work

**Disclosing the adoption gap first.** Rachel opened with the miss. This is the single highest-leverage decision in the session. A CFO's team reviews programme metrics independently — the adoption gap was not a secret. Rachel's choice to disclose it in her first 30 seconds converted a potential ambush into a demonstration of professional maturity.

**The sensitivity table.** Rachel had modelled 70% and 80% adoption scenarios before the meeting. David Torres's question about 70% was not on the prepared agenda. Rachel answered it in real time with specific numbers because she had done the work. CFOs are pattern-matching for programme leads who know their own numbers — and for those who don't.

**The pro/con structure for the governing question.** When Patricia asked "should we continue?", Rachel explicitly presented the case against before making her recommendation to continue. This structure is counterintuitive — most programme leads instinctively present only the case for their own programme. But explicitly presenting the case against is what signals independence and earns trust.

**Operational preparation on the CIO's question.** Rachel had already confirmed the change management pathway with Lena's team before the meeting. When Lena raised the change process question, Rachel had a specific answer and a draft change request ready to share. This kind of operational preparedness signals that the programme team is not just strategically competent but execution-ready.

**The month eight commitment.** Rachel made a specific forward commitment: if the corrective action does not show measurable improvement by month eight, she will recommend pausing. This commitment is uncomfortable to make — it creates accountability. It is also exactly what differentiates a trustworthy programme lead from one who manages upward.
