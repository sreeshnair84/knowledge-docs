# Module 14 — Mentoring & Mastery

This is the capstone module of the Enterprise Architect Masterclass. Everything you have studied across the previous 13 modules converges here into a unified practice: the ability to think, communicate, and act as a trusted Enterprise Architect. This module is designed both as a synthesis and as a guide for the years of professional practice that follow.

---

## The Mentor's Perspective

### How an Experienced Enterprise Architect Thinks Differently

A technical architect asks: *"What is the best technology for this problem?"*

An enterprise architect asks: *"What business outcome does the organisation need, and which combination of technology, process, people, and investment most reliably delivers it?"*

That shift — from technology-first to outcome-first — is the defining transition from technical to enterprise architecture. It sounds simple. It is not. It requires years of deliberate practice to internalise, because every instinct built during a career in technology pulls in the opposite direction.

!!! note "The Instinct Problem"
    Technical architects are trained to solve elegantly. Enterprise architects are trained to decide wisely. Elegance without business wisdom is a liability at the enterprise level — it produces architecturally beautiful systems that nobody funds, nobody adopts, and nobody maintains.

The experienced EA holds several mental models simultaneously:

- **The financial model**: what does this cost over five years, and what does it return?
- **The political model**: who gains power, who loses it, and who controls the budget?
- **The risk model**: what can go wrong, how likely is it, and what is the cost of failure?
- **The timing model**: is now the right moment, or does a better window open in 12 months?
- **The capability model**: does the organisation have the people and processes to absorb this change?

None of these models are taught in technical certification programmes. They are learned by observing experienced architects, by failing in controlled environments, and by mentors who explain *why* a decision was made, not just *what* the decision was.

### The Mental Model Shift

| Dimension | Technical Architect Mindset | Enterprise Architect Mindset |
|-----------|----------------------------|------------------------------|
| Primary question | What is the best solution? | What is the best outcome? |
| Success metric | Technical correctness | Business value delivered |
| Time horizon | This sprint, this release | 3–5 years |
| Risk tolerance | Low (avoids unknowns) | Calibrated (accepts managed risk) |
| Primary audience | Engineering teams | Executives, boards, investment committees |
| Relationship to vendors | Evaluator | Strategic partner or challenger |
| View of governance | Constraint | Enabler of consistency |
| View of politics | Obstacle | Reality to navigate |
| Definition of done | System deployed | Business outcome achieved |

### The Three Questions Every EA Must Answer Before Proposing Anything

Before putting a word on paper, before opening a slide deck, before booking a stakeholder meeting, an enterprise architect must be able to answer three questions clearly and concisely:

**1. So what?**
Why does this matter? What is the business consequence of doing this versus not doing it? If you cannot articulate the business impact in two sentences without using technical language, you are not ready to propose.

**2. Who cares?**
Who is affected — positively or negatively? Which executives will sponsor this? Which business units will resist it? Which teams will be asked to change their behaviour? Understanding the stakeholder landscape before you propose is the difference between a proposal that moves and one that stalls.

**3. What do we do?**
What is the recommended action? Not a menu of options (though options have their place) — a clear, defended recommendation. Executives pay architects to have a view. Presenting three equally weighted options is not architecture; it is a list.

!!! tip "Mentor Insight"
    Experienced EAs answer these three questions in their *heads* before every meeting, not just before every proposal. It becomes a mental habit — a constant filter through which every conversation is processed.

## Career Progression: Junior to Chief Architect

### Overview

The enterprise architecture career ladder has four primary levels. Each level represents not just an increase in seniority but a qualitative change in how the architect thinks, what they focus on, and how they create value.

| Dimension | Junior EA | Senior EA | Principal EA | Chief EA |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|--------------|----------|
| Scope of influence | Team / project | Product / capability | Domain (e.g., data, security) | Enterprise-wide |
| Time horizon | Current quarter | 1–2 years | 2–3 years | 3–5 years |
| Executive engagement | Rarely, via manager | Occasionally, with preparation | Regularly, independently | Continuously, at C-suite level |
| Proposal ownership | Contributes sections | Owns sections | Owns full document | Owns strategy narrative |
| Governance role | Attendee | Contributor | Presenter / reviewer | Chair / sponsor |
| Typical deliverables | Architecture diagrams, assessments | Technology strategies, ADRs | Domain strategies, investment guides | Enterprise strategy, TOM |
| Primary relationships | Development teams, architects | Product owners, programme managers | Domain leaders, senior business stakeholders | C-suite, board, major vendors |
| Typical time to reach level | 0–3 years EA experience | 3–6 years EA experience | 6–10 years EA experience | 10+ years EA experience |

---

### Junior Enterprise Architect (0–3 Years)

**Scope of Influence**: Team and project level. The Junior EA supports existing architecture initiatives rather than leading them.

**Typical Activities and Deliverables**:
- Creating and maintaining architecture diagrams (current state, component, sequence)
- Conducting technology assessments and vendor research
- Writing sections of architecture documents under supervision
- Attending ARB and governance meetings as an observer
- Documenting architecture decisions (ADRs) for review

**Executive Engagement**: Rarely direct. The Junior EA presents to technical leads, not executives. Their work feeds upward through more senior architects.

**Governance Role**: Observer and contributor. Attends ARB and steering committees to learn, not to lead. May be asked to present technical findings but rarely frames the business context.

**Common Mistakes and How to Grow Past Them**:

!!! warning "Common Junior EA Mistakes"
    - **Over-engineering**: proposing technically perfect solutions without regard for cost, timeline, or organisational capacity to absorb change.
    - **Ignoring stakeholders**: focusing on the technology problem rather than understanding who is affected and what they need.
    - **Confusing activity with progress**: producing many diagrams and documents without asking whether any of it is influencing a decision.
    - **Avoiding ambiguity**: wanting clear requirements before starting, rather than learning to work with incomplete information.

**Growth Path**: The transition from Junior to Senior comes when the architect shifts their primary question from "what is the right technology?" to "what does this business need, and how does technology serve that?" This usually requires deliberate exposure to business stakeholders, financial conversations, and governance bodies.

---

### Senior Enterprise Architect (3–6 Years)

**Scope of Influence**: Product and capability level. The Senior EA owns a technology domain or product area and drives its architecture strategy.

**Typical Activities and Deliverables**:
- Owning technology strategy for a product, platform, or capability
- Writing full architecture assessments and recommendations
- Presenting to programme boards and product leadership
- Contributing to investment business cases
- Reviewing and approving architecture decisions in their domain
- Mentoring junior architects

**Executive Engagement**: Occasional and prepared. The Senior EA presents to Directors and VPs, usually with rehearsal and manager review. They are beginning to develop their own executive communication style.

**Governance Role**: Active contributor. Presents at ARB, sits on working groups, and may chair domain-level governance forums.

**Common Mistakes and How to Grow Past Them**:

!!! warning "Common Senior EA Mistakes"
    - **Domain insularity**: becoming expert in one domain while underestimating the importance of cross-domain thinking.
    - **Consensus-seeking**: wanting everyone to agree before moving forward, which paralises decisions in politically complex organisations.
    - **Technical credibility dependence**: relying on deep technical knowledge for authority rather than developing business credibility.
    - **Underestimating the financial dimension**: writing proposals without a compelling financial narrative.

**Growth Path**: The transition to Principal requires the architect to operate across domain boundaries, influence without authority, and command the financial conversation as fluently as the technical one.

---

### Principal Enterprise Architect (6–10 Years)

**Scope of Influence**: Domain or programme level. The Principal EA shapes the architecture of a major domain (data, security, integration, AI) or leads a cross-cutting architecture programme.

**Typical Activities and Deliverables**:
- Owning domain architecture strategy (3-year roadmap)
- Leading investment business cases end-to-end
- Driving cross-domain architecture decisions
- Building architecture capability (frameworks, standards, training)
- Engaging C-1 executives (Directors, VPs, SVPs) regularly
- Representing architecture at Investment Committee or equivalent

**Executive Engagement**: Regular and independent. The Principal EA meets with senior executives without manager involvement, develops relationships with business sponsors, and is trusted to represent the architecture perspective in high-stakes discussions.

**Governance Role**: Leadership. Chairs or co-chairs governance bodies, sets standards, resolves escalations, and ensures architecture decisions are documented and tracked.

**Common Mistakes and How to Grow Past Them**:

!!! warning "Common Principal EA Mistakes"
    - **Strategic without being operational**: producing elegant strategies that never translate into actionable roadmaps.
    - **Authority without accountability**: influencing decisions without owning outcomes.
    - **Neglecting political capital**: spending capital pushing for the right answer without building the relationships that make the right answer achievable.
    - **Underinvesting in people**: focusing on architecture deliverables rather than developing the next generation of architects.

**Growth Path**: The transition to Chief requires the architect to operate at the enterprise level — across all domains, across all business units — and to be recognised by C-suite executives as a strategic peer, not a technical advisor.

---

### Chief Enterprise Architect (10+ Years)

**Scope of Influence**: Enterprise-wide. The Chief EA shapes the technology and architecture strategy of the entire organisation.

**Typical Activities and Deliverables**:
- Owning the Enterprise Architecture strategy, framework, and roadmap
- Advising the board and C-suite on technology investment, risk, and capability
- Setting architecture principles and governance standards
- Leading the EA team and community of practice
- Representing the organisation externally (industry bodies, standards groups, vendors)
- Owning the Target Operating Model for technology

**Executive Engagement**: Continuous and strategic. The Chief EA is a trusted advisor to the CTO, CDO, CISO, and CFO. They present to the board. They are involved in major vendor negotiations, mergers and acquisitions due diligence, and regulatory engagement.

**Governance Role**: Sponsor and chair. Sets the governance framework, resolves the most complex escalations, and is ultimately accountable for architecture quality across the enterprise.

**Common Mistakes and How to Grow Past Them**:

!!! warning "Common Chief EA Mistakes"
    - **Losing touch with technical reality**: becoming so focused on strategy that they lose the ability to evaluate whether a technical claim is credible.
    - **Bureaucratic governance**: building governance processes that are so heavy they impede delivery.
    - **Confusing influence with authority**: assuming that seniority translates to automatic buy-in.
    - **Neglecting succession**: failing to develop Principal architects who can eventually succeed them.

## "How an Experienced EA Thinks" — Eight Scenarios

### Scenario 1: When a Business Leader Demands the Wrong Technology

**The situation**: A CFO has read an article about blockchain and is convinced the organisation's supply chain problem can be solved with it. They want a proposal on their desk in two weeks.

**How the experienced EA thinks**:

The EA does not refuse. They do not comply immediately either. They schedule a discovery conversation with the CFO and ask questions that reveal the underlying business need: what problem are we actually solving? What does success look like? What happens if we do nothing?

In that conversation, the EA often discovers that the real problem is data reconciliation between partners — which may be solvable with a far simpler API integration. The EA then presents a brief on "approaches to solving the partner data reconciliation problem," with blockchain as one option (and a clear explanation of when it is and is not appropriate), alongside two or three alternatives with comparative cost and risk profiles.

The experienced EA never says "that's wrong." They say "let's make sure we're solving the right problem first."

---

### Scenario 2: When the Budget is Half What the Architecture Needs

**The situation**: The approved investment is $2M. The minimum credible implementation requires $4M.

**How the experienced EA thinks**:

The EA resists the temptation to scope-cut until the solution is unworkable. Instead, they ask: what outcome can $2M actually achieve? They design a phased approach in which the first $2M delivers a standalone, meaningful result — not just a foundation that requires future funding to be useful. They present two options: the full $4M programme with full value, and a $2M Phase 1 that delivers a defined subset of value with a credible path to Phase 2.

They also explore whether some costs can be restructured (CapEx to OpEx, vendor financing, shared funding across business units) to make the full investment achievable without requiring more from a single budget holder.

---

### Scenario 3: When the Executive Committee is Split on a Decision

**The situation**: The CTO wants to build a proprietary AI platform. The CDO wants to buy from a major cloud vendor. The CFO is uncommitted. There is no consensus.

**How the experienced EA thinks**:

The EA recognises that the decision is not primarily technical — it is political. They do not try to force a technical answer. Instead, they frame the decision around the criteria that matter to each executive: the CTO's concern for control and differentiation, the CDO's concern for data governance and speed, the CFO's concern for cost and risk.

The EA prepares a structured decision framework that surfaces the implications of each option against those criteria, with explicit assumptions that each party can challenge. The goal is not to win the argument but to create shared clarity about what is actually being decided and why it matters.

Often, the EA finds that the disagreement is not about the platform at all — it is about who controls the AI programme. Resolving the governance question resolves the technology question.

---

### Scenario 4: When a Vendor Promises Something Architecturally Unsound

**The situation**: A major vendor has promised an integration capability in Q3 that the EA knows is not on their product roadmap. The business has designed a programme around it.

**How the experienced EA thinks**:

The EA documents their concern clearly and early. They request a formal written commitment from the vendor — not a slide, not a verbal assurance — and brief their sponsor on the risk. They design a contingency into the programme architecture so that if the vendor fails to deliver, the programme does not fail catastrophically.

The experienced EA does not create a political crisis (which would damage their credibility) but they do not stay silent (which would make them complicit in a risk that was foreseeable). They raise the flag, propose a mitigation, and ensure the decision-maker is informed.

---

### Scenario 5: When a Previous EA Left Behind an Undocumented System

**The situation**: A critical production system processes $200M of transactions annually. There is no documentation. The architect who built it left 18 months ago.

**How the experienced EA thinks**:

The EA begins with a pragmatic risk assessment: what is the probability of this system causing an outage, and what is the cost if it does? They do not immediately recommend a rewrite (a common and expensive mistake). Instead, they commission a structured discovery process — a combination of code analysis, log analysis, and interviews with people who have operated the system — to build a working architecture diagram.

They then assess the specific risks: single points of failure, undocumented dependencies, security gaps, and licensing issues. They prioritise mitigation by risk severity, not by architectural elegance. The first goal is to reduce risk; modernisation comes later, with funding and a business case.

---

### Scenario 6: When the Programme is Failing and They Call You In to Fix It

**The situation**: A large transformation programme is 18 months in, 40% over budget, and has delivered no production capability. You are asked to conduct an architecture review.

**How the experienced EA thinks**:

The EA's first instinct is to listen, not to diagnose. They spend the first week meeting people across the programme — not just architects and project managers, but developers, business analysts, and business stakeholders — asking what is working, what is not, and what they would change.

What they typically find is not an architecture problem. It is a governance problem: unclear decision-making, changing requirements, lack of business ownership, or a technically correct architecture that was never connected to a realistic delivery plan.

The EA produces a findings document that is honest but constructive. They resist the temptation to criticise everything that was done before them. They identify the two or three root causes that, if addressed, would unlock progress — and they recommend a 90-day stabilisation plan before any larger restructuring.

---

### Scenario 7: When You Know the Right Answer but Nobody Will Listen

**The situation**: Every technical signal points to retiring a legacy platform. The business owner refuses because they fear losing control of their customisations.

**How the experienced EA thinks**:

The EA recognises that "nobody will listen" is rarely true. What is true is that the right answer has not been framed in terms that matter to the people with the authority to act. The business owner's real concern is not the technology — it is continuity of their customisations and fear of disruption.

The EA addresses the fear directly. They commission a detailed analysis of which customisations exist, which are genuinely necessary, and which can be replicated in the target platform. They present a migration approach that specifically protects the business owner's legitimate concerns.

They also build a coalition: CFO cares about cost; CISO cares about security risk; CTO cares about technical debt. The EA creates a shared view of the cost of inaction across all three dimensions. The business owner is then not fighting the EA — they are managing a risk that multiple executives are now aware of.

---

### Scenario 8: When a Competitor's New Product Changes Everything Overnight

**The situation**: A competitor has launched an AI-powered product that appears to make your organisation's roadmap obsolete. The executive team is in emergency mode.

**How the experienced EA thinks**:

The experienced EA brings calm analysis to a room full of panic. Their first act is to assess, not to react. They spend 48 hours analysing the competitor's product: what does it actually do, what architectural choices does it reflect, what does it require in terms of data and infrastructure, and what regulatory considerations apply?

They then prepare a brief that separates fact from perception: what is genuinely threatening, what can be responded to within the existing roadmap, and what requires a strategic decision. They propose a structured response: a 30-day competitive intelligence programme and a 90-day strategic options assessment — rather than an immediate pivot that abandons in-flight investments.

The experienced EA's value in this moment is not to have the answer. It is to slow the panic, impose structure on the problem, and give the executive team something credible to act on.

## 90-Day Enterprise Architect Practice Plan

This plan is designed for a technical architect making the transition to enterprise architecture. It is structured around deliberate exposure, observation, and progressively greater contribution.

### Weeks 1–2: Orientation

**Goal**: Understand the organisation, its portfolio, and the EA function.

| Activity | Output |
|----------|--------|
| Read the current Enterprise Architecture strategy document | Summary of current-state architecture and roadmap |
| Read the last three ARB decision records | Understanding of governance patterns and current issues |
| Review the organisation's strategy document and annual report | Summary of top 3 strategic priorities |
| Meet your manager and agree on 90-day objectives | Written objectives document |
| Request an introduction to five key stakeholders (business, technology, finance) | Stakeholder map (names, roles, interests) |
| Map the current technology portfolio: systems, platforms, vendors | Initial portfolio inventory |

!!! note "Week 1–2 Mindset"
    The goal of the first two weeks is to listen, not to contribute. Every organisation has context that is invisible from the outside. Resist the temptation to offer solutions before you understand the problem landscape.

### Weeks 3–4: Observe

**Goal**: Understand how decisions are actually made in this organisation.

| Activity | Output |
|----------|--------|
| Attend one full ARB meeting as an observer | Notes on decision-making patterns, key participants, common objections |
| Attend one Investment Committee or equivalent | Notes on how business cases are evaluated |
| Attend one programme steering committee | Notes on how programmes report progress and manage risk |
| Shadow a senior EA through one full working day | Notes on how they spend their time and manage relationships |
| Interview three business stakeholders about their biggest technology frustrations | Stakeholder insight summary |

!!! tip "The Observation Rule"
    When observing, take notes on *why* decisions were made, not just *what* was decided. The reasoning behind a decision tells you far more about the organisation than the decision itself.

## The Capstone Challenge

The Capstone Challenge integrates all 14 modules into a single, cohesive EA engagement. Choose a real organisation you know well, or design a detailed fictitious enterprise. Complete each part in sequence — each part builds on the previous one.

### Part 1: AI Maturity Assessment (Modules 1, 5)

Assess the current AI maturity of your chosen enterprise.

- Define the organisation: industry, size, geographic footprint, primary business model
- Map the current technology portfolio (key systems, data assets, cloud posture)
- Apply an AI maturity model to rate the organisation across five dimensions: Strategy, Data, Technology, Talent, Governance
- Produce a one-page maturity heatmap with a written narrative explaining each rating
- Identify the top three constraints on AI maturity in this organisation

**Deliverable**: 3–5 page AI maturity assessment

---

### Part 2: AI Transformation Opportunity Identification (Modules 2, 12)

Identify and prioritise the highest-value AI transformation opportunity.

- Generate a longlist of at least 10 potential AI use cases for this enterprise
- Apply a prioritisation framework (strategic value, feasibility, speed to value, risk) to score each
- Select the top opportunity and describe: the problem it solves, the value it creates, the data and technology it requires, and the risks it introduces
- Validate the opportunity by designing a discovery questionnaire you would use with the business stakeholder

**Deliverable**: Use case longlist with scoring, plus 2-page opportunity brief for the selected use case

---

### Part 3: Business Case (Module 6)

Build the business case for your selected opportunity.

- Complete all 11 sections of the Executive Business Case Template
- Articulate the strategic alignment, current-state problem, proposed solution, financial model, and risks
- Include a benefits realisation plan with quarterly milestones
- Include an alternative options analysis (minimum two alternatives)

**Deliverable**: Complete business case document (minimum 10 pages)

---

### Part 4: Target Architecture (Module 9)

Design the target architecture for your selected opportunity.

- Produce a current-state architecture diagram (business, data, application, and technology layers)
- Produce a target-state architecture diagram
- Identify the key architecture decisions and document them as ADRs (minimum three)
- Define the integration architecture
- Identify and assess architecture risks

**Deliverable**: Architecture document with diagrams and three ADRs

---

### Part 5: Executive Proposal (Module 7)

Write the executive proposal for your selected opportunity.

- Use the Enterprise Proposal Playbook to structure the proposal
- Frame the proposal for a C-suite audience
- Answer "So what?", "Who cares?", and "What do we do?" explicitly
- Include a one-page executive summary
- Limit the main proposal to 8–10 pages

**Deliverable**: Complete executive proposal with executive summary

---

### Part 6: Financial Model (Module 8)

Build the financial model supporting your proposal.

- Complete a 5-year TCO model (current state vs. target state)
- Calculate NPV, IRR, and payback period
- Build a sensitivity analysis with three scenarios (conservative, base, optimistic)
- Produce a one-page financial summary suitable for CFO review

**Deliverable**: Financial model (spreadsheet) plus one-page financial summary

---

### Part 7: Governance Approach (Module 10)

Plan the governance approach for your programme.

- Define the governance structure: which bodies are involved, what decisions they make, and how escalation works
- Produce a RACI for the programme's key decisions
- Design the ARB submission for your architecture
- Define the AI governance approach: responsible AI principles applicable to your use case, risk assessment, and monitoring plan

**Deliverable**: Governance plan (5–7 pages) including RACI and ARB submission

---

### Part 8: Executive Presentation (Module 11)

Prepare and deliver the investment approval pitch.

- Use the Executive Presentation Template (investment approval pitch, 10 slides)
- Present to a real or simulated executive panel
- Prepare for the three most likely executive objections
- Produce a Q&A document with answers to 10 anticipated questions

**Deliverable**: 10-slide presentation, Q&A document, and (if possible) video recording of the delivery

## Final Mastery Checklist

You are ready to practice as an Enterprise Architect when you can independently do all of the following:

- [ ] 1. Explain an AI transformation opportunity in one sentence to a CEO with no technical background
- [ ] 2. Map an organisation's current AI maturity and identify the top three constraints
- [ ] 3. Prioritise a longlist of AI use cases using a structured scoring model
- [ ] 4. Write all 11 sections of an executive business case without a template
- [ ] 5. Build a 5-year TCO financial model and calculate NPV and IRR
- [ ] 6. Explain NPV, IRR, and payback period to a CFO and answer their follow-up questions
- [ ] 7. Design a target architecture that spans business, data, application, and technology layers
- [ ] 8. Write an Architecture Decision Record that will still make sense to someone reading it in five years
- [ ] 9. Structure a governance approach for a complex AI programme, including responsible AI controls
- [ ] 10. Write an executive proposal that a non-technical reader can follow and act on
- [ ] 11. Deliver a 10-slide investment approval pitch to an executive panel and handle objections
- [ ] 12. Run a discovery workshop with five or more business stakeholders
- [ ] 13. Navigate a politically complex stakeholder situation without losing credibility with any party
- [ ] 14. Identify when a vendor is overpromising and communicate the risk without creating a crisis
- [ ] 15. Chair an Architecture Review Board session
- [ ] 16. Conduct a peer review of another architect's proposal and deliver useful, constructive feedback
- [ ] 17. Mentor a junior architect through their first 90 days
- [ ] 18. Read a competitor's new product announcement and produce a structured competitive assessment within 48 hours

- [ ] 20. Complete all eight parts of the Capstone Challenge to the standard required for an investment committee submission

!!! tip "On Mastery"
    Mastery is not a destination — it is a practice. The architects who grow the most are those who remain genuinely curious, who seek feedback relentlessly, and who believe that every engagement, however routine it appears, contains something worth learning. The checklist above is a starting point, not a finish line.
