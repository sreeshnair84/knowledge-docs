# Deliverable Templates 11–20: Consulting & Practice Tools

These ten templates support the full arc of an EA consulting engagement, from discovery through delivery, practice management, and professional development. All templates are copy-paste-ready. Remove instructional admonition blocks before client delivery.

## Template 11 — Consulting Workshop Toolkit

!!! info "When to Use"
    Use this toolkit to plan and run discovery workshops, design sprints, and alignment sessions. The full-day agenda below is a starting structure — adapt timing to the specific outcome you need. Always distribute the pre-read and objectives 48 hours before the session.

### 11.1 Full-Day Discovery Workshop Agenda (9:00am – 5:00pm)

```
DISCOVERY WORKSHOP AGENDA
Client:    ___________________________
Date:      ___________________________
Facilitator: _________________________
Venue / Link: _______________________
Participants: ________________________

PRE-READ (distribute 48 hrs before)
- Workshop objectives and expected outputs
- Current-state overview (1-pager max)
- Pre-work: each participant brings 3 "pain points" and 3 "what good looks like" statements

---

09:00 – 09:15  WELCOME AND SCENE-SETTING
  Facilitator: Introductions; review agenda; confirm outputs; agree ground rules
  Ground rules: one conversation, phones away, "yes and" not "yes but", parking lot honoured

09:15 – 09:45  SESSION 1: CONTEXT AND STRATEGY ALIGNMENT
  Objective: Shared understanding of strategic direction and constraints
  Method:    Facilitator presents 5-minute context frame; round-robin responses
  Output:    Agreed strategic context statement (1–2 sentences, flip chart / Miro)

09:45 – 10:30  SESSION 2: CURRENT STATE PAIN POINTS
  Objective: Surface and prioritise key current-state pain points
  Method:    Silent brainstorming (10 min) → share out → affinity mapping → dot voting
  Output:    Top 5–7 pain points ranked by participant votes

10:30 – 10:45  BREAK

10:45 – 12:00  SESSION 3: FUTURE STATE VISIONING
  Objective: Define what "great" looks like across key dimensions
  Method:    Small groups (3–4 people) per dimension → gallery walk → consolidation
  Dimensions: [Customise — e.g. Data, Customer Experience, Operations, Technology]
  Output:    Future state statements per dimension (posted on walls / Miro frames)

12:00 – 13:00  LUNCH (working lunch optional — use for informal conversations)

13:00 – 13:45  SESSION 4: GAP AND CHALLENGE IDENTIFICATION
  Objective: Identify gaps between current and future state
  Method:    Return to morning outputs; facilitated gap mapping on whiteboard
  Output:    Gap register (current state → future state → gap description per dimension)

13:45 – 14:30  SESSION 5: INITIATIVE IDEATION
  Objective: Generate candidate initiatives to close priority gaps
  Method:    Individual sticky notes (5 min) → table groups cluster and name → share back
  Output:    Long list of candidate initiatives (20–40 ideas typical)

14:30 – 14:45  BREAK

14:45 – 15:30  SESSION 6: INITIATIVE PRIORITISATION
  Objective: Prioritise initiatives by impact and feasibility
  Method:    2×2 matrix (Impact vs. Feasibility); facilitator plots, group adjusts
  Output:    Prioritised initiative list — top quadrant = focus area for roadmap

15:30 – 16:15  SESSION 7: ROADMAP SHAPE AND DEPENDENCIES
  Objective: Draft a high-level phased roadmap
  Method:    Facilitated whiteboard (facilitator draws; participants direct)
  Output:    Draft 3-phase roadmap on whiteboard (photo captured)

16:15 – 16:45  SESSION 8: DECISIONS, ACTIONS, AND OWNERS
  Objective: Close out the day with clear next steps
  Method:    Facilitator reads back decision log and action register; participants confirm
  Output:    Confirmed decision log + action register with owners and due dates

16:45 – 17:00  CLOSE AND FEEDBACK
  Method:    One word round-robin to close; 5-minute written feedback form
  Facilitator distributes: written summary within 2 business days
```

### 11.2 Facilitation Techniques

#### Dot Voting

Each participant receives 5 sticky dots (physical) or 5 votes (digital). Participants place all votes on items they consider most important — they may stack votes on a single item. Count dots to produce a ranked list. Use for: prioritisation of pain points, initiative selection, risk ranking.

#### Silent Brainstorming

Each participant writes ideas on sticky notes independently (no talking) for 5–10 minutes. No editing; quantity over quality. Then participants share out one by one. This prevents groupthink and gives equal voice to introverts and junior participants.

#### Affinity Mapping

Spread all sticky notes on a surface. Participants (silently or with minimal discussion) move notes into clusters of related ideas. Name each cluster with a header card. Clusters become themes. Use after silent brainstorming to structure output.

#### MURAL / Miro Instructions

| Step | Action |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | Create a board before the session; share the link in the pre-read |
| 2 | Set up frames for each session — one frame per output |
| 3 | Use the sticky note tool for brainstorming (assign a colour per participant) |
| 4 | Use the voting timer (Miro: "Vote" feature; MURAL: "Vote" mode) |
| 5 | Lock completed frames so outputs are not accidentally moved |
| 6 | Export the board as PDF at end of day and distribute with session notes |

### 11.3 Virtual Workshop Considerations

| Challenge | Mitigation |
| --- | --- |
| Participant disengagement | Max 90 minutes without a break; use polls and chat prompts every 10–15 min |
| Domination by vocal participants | Use silent brainstorming and structured round-robin, not open discussion |
| Technical failures | Test platform 24 hrs before; have a backup video link ready; designate a tech co-host |
| Breakout room facilitation | Pre-assign groups; place a prompt card (slide) in each breakout room; visit each room once |
| Building rapport without in-person cues | Open with 2-minute check-in activity ("one word for how you're feeling today") |
| Capturing outputs | Designate a dedicated note-taker; use Miro/MURAL so outputs are digital-native |

## Template 12 — Discovery Questionnaire Library

!!! info "When to Use"
    Use these questions in structured interviews, pre-workshop surveys, or as a guide for unstructured discovery conversations. Select 8–12 questions per stakeholder rather than running all 50 in a single session. Adapt wording to match the stakeholder's level.

### Category A — Business Strategy (10 Questions)

1. What are your organisation's top three strategic priorities for the next two to three years, and how is technology expected to contribute to each?
2. What does success look like for your business in five years? How would you measure it?
3. Where are your most significant competitive threats coming from — incumbents, new entrants, or technology disruption?
4. What capabilities does your organisation need to build, buy, or partner to achieve its strategy?
5. How are investment decisions made at the executive level, and what criteria matter most?
6. Which business processes are most critical to your competitive differentiation versus which are commodity?
7. What would have to be true for your organisation to double in scale over the next five years?
8. Are there regulatory, geopolitical, or macroeconomic forces that significantly shape your strategic options?
9. How aligned is your leadership team on strategic direction? Where are the points of tension?
10. If you had unlimited budget for one technology investment this year, what would it be and why?

### Category B — Technology Landscape (10 Questions)

1. How would you describe your current technology landscape — modern, aging, mixed? What is your biggest technical liability?
2. What percentage of your applications are on-premises, in a public cloud, or in a private cloud? Is that proportion changing?
3. Where are your most significant integration challenges — which systems are hardest to connect and why?
4. What does your vendor landscape look like? Are there consolidation opportunities, or is multi-vendor diversity intentional?
5. How do you currently manage technical debt? Is there a formal programme, informal backlog, or is it not tracked?
6. What is your approach to build versus buy versus SaaS? Has that changed in the last three years?
7. How mature is your DevOps and delivery capability — from idea to production, how long does a typical change take?
8. What are your most critical non-functional requirements — availability, performance, security — and are you meeting them today?
9. How do you manage your technology roadmap? Who owns it, and how often is it refreshed?
10. What technology decisions made in the last five years are you most proud of, and which do you most regret?

### Category C — Data and AI Readiness (10 Questions)

1. How would you rate your organisation's data maturity — from ad hoc and siloed to data-driven and governed?
2. Where does your most valuable data live today, and who has access to it?
3. Do you have a single source of truth for your most critical business metrics? If not, where do the disagreements typically arise?
4. What are your biggest data quality pain points, and what is the business impact when data quality fails?
5. Have you deployed any AI or machine learning models in production? If so, describe the use case and the outcome.
6. What AI use cases are you currently exploring or piloting? What is blocking you from moving faster?
7. How are decisions made about what data can be used for AI training, and who oversees that process?
8. What does your data governance look like today — is there a data catalogue, data owners, data quality monitoring?
9. How prepared is your organisation to comply with AI-specific regulations such as the EU AI Act or sector-specific AI guidelines?
10. If I told you we could give every employee a personal AI assistant tomorrow, what would be the biggest risk?

### Category D — Governance Maturity (10 Questions)

1. How are technology architecture decisions made today? Is there a formal review body, or does it happen informally?
2. How are IT and the business aligned on investment priorities? Where does misalignment typically occur?
3. Do you have documented architecture principles? When were they last reviewed?
4. How do you manage vendor risk at an enterprise level — is there a vendor register, risk scoring, and regular reviews?
5. When a project goes off-track, what does the escalation path look like? How effective is it?
6. How are cybersecurity and technology risk managed — separately or as part of an integrated enterprise risk framework?
7. How does your organisation handle technology standards — do you have approved technology lists, and are they enforced?
8. What does your technology assurance or audit function review, and how does its output feed back into governance?
9. How are architecture decisions documented and communicated across the organisation?
10. If a new team started a technology project today without going through formal governance, what would actually happen?

### Category E — Organisational Readiness (10 Questions)

1. How would you describe the relationship between IT and the business — collaborative partner, order-taker, or something else?
2. What is your current approach to technology talent — build internally, hire externally, or lean on partners?
3. Where are your most significant technology skill gaps today, and how are you addressing them?
4. How does your organisation approach change management when deploying new technology?
5. How is the technology function structured — centralised, federated, product-aligned, or hybrid?
6. What is the cultural attitude toward risk and experimentation in your organisation?
7. How does leadership model and signal the importance of technology and data to the wider organisation?
8. What happened the last time a major technology change programme did not go as planned? What did the organisation learn?
9. How do you measure the productivity and performance of your technology function?
10. If you could change one thing about how your organisation approaches technology, what would it be?

## Template 13 — Executive Presentation Templates

### (a) Investment Approval Pitch — 10-Slide Structure

!!! info "Format Notes"
    Slide time: approximately 3 minutes per slide for a 30-minute pitch. Leave 10 minutes for Q&A. Deck should be readable without narration — executives often pre-read before the meeting.

| Slide | Title | Key Content | Design Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Cover | Investment name; presenter name and title; date; confidentiality classification | Clean; logo; single hero image relevant to the technology |
| 2 | The Problem We Are Solving | 2–3 sentences describing the business pain; quantify if possible ("we lose $Xm per year to...") | No bullet lists; large statement text; support with one metric |
| 3 | Why Now | The urgency driver — regulatory deadline, competitive move, operational event | Timeline graphic showing the window of opportunity closing |
| 4 | Proposed Solution | One-sentence description of the solution; what it does, not how it works | Process diagram or solution overview — one visual only |
| 5 | Strategic Alignment | Map the investment to 2–3 named strategic objectives | Visual alignment map; use the organisation's own strategic language |
| 6 | Financial Case | NPV, ROI, payback period; 5-year cost vs. benefit chart | Waterfall or bar chart; single numbers prominent; avoid tables |
| 7 | Risk and Mitigations | Top 3 risks with RAG rating and specific mitigations | Traffic light table; keep it honest — executives distrust risk-free cases |
| 8 | Delivery Approach | High-level phased delivery plan with major milestones | Simple Gantt or phase diagram; 3–4 phases maximum |
| 9 | What We Are Asking For | Precise ask: budget figure, headcount, decisions needed, by when | Single clear request; avoid burying the ask in caveats |
| 10 | Appendix / Backup | Detailed financials, technical architecture, vendor assessments | Label clearly as appendix; do not present unless asked |

### (b) Board AI Readiness Update — 6-Slide Structure

| Slide | Title | Key Content | Design Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Cover | "AI Readiness Update — [Quarter] [Year]"; Board-level confidential | Minimal; date prominent |
| 2 | Where We Stand | Current AI maturity score or assessment summary; what has changed since last update | Radar chart or maturity dial; use consistent scoring scale across updates |
| 3 | AI Initiatives in Flight | Status of each active AI initiative (RAG status, key milestone, risk flag) | Table: Initiative / Status / Next Milestone / Owner / Risk |
| 4 | Responsible AI and Governance | Policy status; any incidents; regulatory developments | Simple status table; flag any open regulatory gaps |
| 5 | Strategic Opportunities | 2–3 AI opportunities the board should be aware of; emerging use cases | Forward-looking; link to competitive context |
| 6 | Board Decision Required | Specific items needing board endorsement, noting or approval | Numbered list with clear decision language; no ambiguity |

### (c) ARB Architecture Presentation — 8-Slide Structure

| Slide | Title | Key Content | Design Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Cover | Submission title; sponsor; ARB date; ARB reference number | Standard ARB template |
| 2 | Business Context | Problem being solved; business driver; urgency | Two sentences maximum per point |
| 3 | Solution Overview | What is being proposed at a conceptual level | Single solution diagram — no code, no protocol detail |
| 4 | Current State Architecture | Relevant scope of current architecture | Architecture diagram; highlight what changes |
| 5 | Target State Architecture | Proposed architecture; annotated with key decisions | Architecture diagram; annotate decisions with decision numbers |
| 6 | Standards Alignment | Map against architecture principles and technology standards | Table: Principle / Compliant / Compliant with Exception / Non-Compliant |
| 7 | Risk and Issues | Top 3 architecture risks with owner and mitigation | RAG table; keep honest |
| 8 | ARB Decision Requested | Specific decision sought; draft ADR reference; conditions already known | Numbered ask; provide draft ADR as appendix |

## Template 14 — Proposal Review Checklists

### (a) Self-Review Checklist (25 Items)

Complete this before handing the proposal to a peer reviewer. Be honest — partial credit does not serve the client or the win rate.

**Executive Summary (5 items)**

- [ ] 1. The executive summary can stand alone — a busy executive who reads only this page understands the problem, the solution, and the ask
- [ ] 2. The client's own words and priorities are reflected (not generic consulting language)
- [ ] 3. The recommended approach is stated clearly, not implied
- [ ] 4. The investment and timeline are stated
- [ ] 5. The most compelling differentiator is visible in the first paragraph

**Problem and Solution (5 items)**

- [ ] 6. The problem statement is specific to this client — not a generic industry statement
- [ ] 7. The root cause is identified, not just the symptom
- [ ] 8. The solution is directly mapped to the problem — no unexplained logic leap
- [ ] 9. Jargon is either defined or removed
- [ ] 10. The "so what" of each major claim is explicit (not left to the reader)

**Approach and Methodology (5 items)**

- [ ] 11. The delivery phases are named and the purpose of each is clear
- [ ] 12. Deliverables for each phase are listed with enough specificity to set expectations
- [ ] 13. The client's expected involvement at each phase is stated
- [ ] 14. Assumptions and out-of-scope items are clearly listed
- [ ] 15. The team structure and roles are shown (not just names and titles)

**Commercial (5 items)**

- [ ] 16. The total investment figure is easy to find on first read
- [ ] 17. What is included in the price is explicit
- [ ] 18. What is not included (and what might trigger a change request) is stated
- [ ] 19. Payment milestones are linked to deliverable completion, not time
- [ ] 20. The validity period of the proposal is stated

**Credibility and Close (5 items)**

- [ ] 21. Relevant case studies or references are included
- [ ] 22. The team's relevant experience is matched to this engagement, not generic bios
- [ ] 23. The next step is stated explicitly with a specific date or action
- [ ] 24. All named individuals have reviewed and approved their sections
- [ ] 25. The document has been spell-checked, grammar-checked, and formatted consistently

### (b) Peer Review Checklist (15 Items)

As a peer reviewer, read the proposal cold — do not ask the author to explain it first.

- [ ] 1. After reading the executive summary only, I can state in one sentence what the client's problem is
- [ ] 2. After reading the executive summary only, I can state in one sentence what we are proposing to do
- [ ] 3. After reading the executive summary only, I know what the investment is
- [ ] 4. The proposal is clearly written for this specific client — it does not feel like a template with names swapped in
- [ ] 5. The recommended approach is logically structured — each phase leads naturally to the next
- [ ] 6. The team proposed is credible for the work described — experience matches the challenge
- [ ] 7. There are no claims the firm cannot substantiate if challenged
- [ ] 8. The commercial terms are fair and realistic — nothing that would cause a client concern on reflection
- [ ] 9. There are no obvious gaps — nothing a client would expect to see that is missing
- [ ] 10. There is no internal jargon or methodology branding that the client would not understand
- [ ] 11. The document is visually consistent and professional — no formatting anomalies
- [ ] 12. Page count is appropriate — not padded, not incomplete
- [ ] 13. The win theme (our single most compelling differentiator) is evident throughout, not just in one section
- [ ] 14. The close is strong — I know exactly what the client should do next and by when
- [ ] 15. I would be comfortable submitting this under my own name

### (c) Final Pre-Submission Checklist (10 Items)

Run this in the last 30 minutes before submission.

- [ ] 1. Document version is correct — not a draft version or named "FINAL_v2_REVISED"
- [ ] 2. Client name and entity name are spelled correctly throughout (including in headers and footers)
- [ ] 3. No previous client names, internal notes, or tracked changes remain in the document
- [ ] 4. File size is appropriate for email delivery (< 10MB unless client has confirmed otherwise)
- [ ] 5. PDF version tested — all fonts embedded, no broken links, all pages visible
- [ ] 6. Submission is addressed to the correct contact at the correct email address
- [ ] 7. Any required forms or cover letters are complete and attached
- [ ] 8. Submission deadline confirmed — including time zone if submitting across geographies
- [ ] 9. Partner or principal sign-off received and documented
- [ ] 10. Confirmation of submission method and receipt process confirmed with client contact

### 15.4 Dependency Notation Guide

| Notation | Meaning | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Arrow `→` | Sequential dependency (B cannot start until A completes) | Data Assessment → Platform Build |
| Bracket `[A requires B]` | Conditional dependency (A can start but cannot complete without B) | GenAI Pilot [requires RAI Framework] |
| Dashed line `- - -` | Parallel track with coordination required | Two use case pilots running simultaneously |
| `*` asterisk | External dependency (vendor, regulator, partner) | `[S*]` Scale requires regulator approval |
| `!` exclamation | Critical path item — delay here delays the programme | `[B!]` Platform build is on the critical path |

## Template 16 — EA Deliverable Templates

### 16.1 Current-State Architecture Document — Table of Contents and Section Guidance

```
CURRENT-STATE ARCHITECTURE DOCUMENT
[Organisation Name]
Version: [X.X] | Classification: [INTERNAL / CONFIDENTIAL]
Prepared by: [Name(s)] | Review Date: [DD MMM YYYY]

TABLE OF CONTENTS

1. Executive Summary
   Purpose: 1-page summary for non-technical readers. State the scope,
   key findings, and top 3–5 architecture concerns. Write last.

2. Scope and Context
   Define what is in scope (which business domains, systems, geographies).
   Include a context diagram showing the organisation and its external interfaces.

3. Business Architecture
   Describe the relevant business capabilities and processes the architecture
   supports. Use a business capability map if available. Note capability gaps.

4. Application Architecture
   Inventory of in-scope applications: system name, owner, technology stack,
   deployment model, lifecycle status, interfaces, data classification.
   Include an application landscape diagram.

5. Data Architecture
   Key data domains, data flows between systems, master data sources,
   known data quality issues, data governance maturity.
   Include a data flow diagram for key processes.

6. Integration Architecture
   Integration patterns in use (point-to-point, ESB, API, event-driven).
   Integration complexity map. Known fragility points.

7. Infrastructure Architecture
   Hosting model (on-prem, cloud, hybrid), network topology,
   key infrastructure components, availability and DR posture.

8. Security Architecture
   Identity and access model, perimeter controls, data protection controls,
   key security gaps and open vulnerabilities (at appropriate classification).

9. Architecture Concerns and Technical Debt Register
   Structured list of identified concerns: ID | Description | Risk Level |
   Impacted Systems | Recommended Action | Owner

10. Architecture Principles Assessment
    Map current state against approved principles.
    Table: Principle | Compliance Status | Gap Description

11. Appendices
    A — Full application inventory (spreadsheet)
    B — Full integration map
    C — Source data and interview list
```

### 16.3 Architecture Decision Record (ADR) — Full Template

See also Template 8.6. This version is the standalone format for use outside of the ARB context.

```markdown
# ADR-[NNN]: [Concise Title — Present Tense, e.g. "Adopt Kafka for Event Streaming"]

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Status | Proposed / Accepted / Deprecated / Superseded |
| Date | DD MMM YYYY |
| Authors | [Names] |
| Reviewers | [Names] |
| Deciders | [Names — those with authority to accept] |
| Supersedes | ADR-NNN (if applicable) |
| Superseded by | ADR-NNN (if applicable) |

## Context

What is the issue motivating this decision? Describe the forces at play —
technical, business, organisational. What will happen if no decision is made?
What constraints exist (cost, time, existing technology, skills)?

## Decision

State the decision in plain language. Begin with "We will..." or "We have
decided to...". Be specific. Include what is being decided AND what is
explicitly not in scope of this decision.

## Rationale

Why is this the best option given the context? Reference evaluation
criteria. Refer to any POC results, benchmarks, or reference architectures.

## Consequences
```

#### Background: Meridian Financial Group

Meridian Financial Group (MFG) is a mid-sized diversified financial services firm operating in retail banking, wealth management, and general insurance. It employs 8,000 people across three countries and manages $120 billion in assets under management.

MFG is under significant pressure from digital-native competitors, regulatory change (including upcoming AI Act obligations), and internal technology debt accumulated over 15 years of acquisition-led growth. Its technology estate includes 340 applications, 60% of which are on-premises, on aging infrastructure. Data is siloed across business units. The firm has no enterprise AI capability.

The CEO has mandated a 3-year technology transformation to become a "data-driven, AI-enabled organisation" while reducing technology costs by 20% and improving system availability from 99.2% to 99.9%.

#### Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I make up additional assumptions?** Yes. State all assumptions explicitly in your appendix. Assessors will not penalise reasonable assumptions that are clearly documented.

**Can I use AI tools?** Yes, with disclosure. Note in your submission which elements were AI-assisted. The analysis, synthesis, and recommendations must be your own.

**Is there a right answer?** No. Assessors are looking for logical consistency, rigour, and the ability to defend your position — not a specific set of recommendations.

**What makes an excellent submission?** Excellent submissions go beyond applying the templates correctly. They surface non-obvious insights, make explicit trade-offs, and produce a recommendation the assessors believe a real client could act on.
