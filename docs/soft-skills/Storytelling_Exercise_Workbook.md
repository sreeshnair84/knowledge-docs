**01  The Frameworks**   STAR Method + 3-Act Structure

## **The STAR Method**

STAR is the gold standard for work stories. It is tight, professional, and keeps you on track.

| | Element | What to include — and how long |
| --- | --- | --- |
| S | Situation | Set the scene. Who, where, when. Max 2 sentences. Resist the urge to over-explain. |
| T | Task | Name the challenge, goal, or problem. Make it feel real — what was at stake? |
| A | Action | Your specific contribution. Use 'I', not 'we'. This is the heart of the story. |
| R | Result | What changed? Quantify if possible. End with the impact, not the activity. |

**Example:**The client was about to cancel. (S) I needed to rebuild trust in a single meeting. (T) I led with their pain points before showing any solution. (A) We retained the account and expanded by 20%. (R)

## **The 3-Act Structure**

Use this when you have more than 90 seconds — quarterly reviews, keynotes, team updates.

| Act | Length | Purpose |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Act 1 — Setup | 20% | Orient the audience. Context, characters, stakes. Keep it tight. |
| Act 2 — Conflict | 60% | The problem, tension, or challenge. This is where attention lives. Never rush. |
| Act 3 — Resolution | 20% | What happened. What you learned. What action follows. |

**Golden Rule:**Most speakers spend 80% of their time on Act 1 (setup) and rush the resolution. Flip it. Your audience wants the conflict and the outcome — not the backstory.

**03  Emotion Mapping**   Balance logic and feeling in every story

Take Story #1 from Section 2. Break it into 6 beats (sentences or moments). Label each beat L (Logic — facts, data, process) or E (Emotion — feeling, tension, stakes). A great story alternates between the two.

**Target pattern:**E → L → E → L → E → L  or  L → E → L → E → L → E.  If you see five L's in a row — add a human moment. If you see five E's — ground it with a fact.
