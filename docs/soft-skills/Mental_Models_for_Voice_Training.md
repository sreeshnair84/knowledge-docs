# **What Are Mental Models?**
A mental model is a framework for thinking — a lens that helps you understand a situation, make better decisions, and solve problems faster. The best speakers in the world don't just practise harder; they think differently about how they learn and grow.

This document maps 8 powerful mental models directly to your 30-day voice training journey, so every hour of practice compounds intelligently.

*Each model below includes what it means, how it applies to your voice training, and exactly when to use it in your plan.*

**01  First Principles Thinking    **Strip everything down to what is actually true

| 01 | First Principles Thinking  Question every assumption |
| --- | --- |
| 01 | What it means: Break any problem down to its most basic, undeniable truths — then reason up from scratch. Don't copy what others do; ask what is actually necessary. Applied to your voice training: Instead of imitating speakers you admire, ask: what does a powerful voice actually require? Answer: breath control, clear articulation, intentional pace, and presence. Build those fundamentals first. Everything else is decoration. |
| 01 | Use this in: Week 1 — Day 1 setup. When you feel stuck, return to the basics. |

**02  Inversion    **Think backwards to move forward

| 02 | Inversion  Ask: how do I guarantee failure? |
| --- | --- |
| 02 | What it means: Instead of only asking 'how do I succeed?', ask 'what would guarantee I fail?' — then systematically avoid those things. Charlie Munger's favourite mental model. Applied to your voice training: Ask yourself: what would make me a terrible public speaker? Answers: rushing through words, filling silence with ums, shallow breathing, no eye contact, no preparation. Now flip every single one — that is your training checklist. |
| 02 | Use this in: Week 1 — Day 5 habit identification. Review your recordings asking 'what bad habit am I keeping?' |

**03  Feedback Loops    **Build a system that teaches itself

| 03 | Feedback Loops  Every action creates a reaction |
| --- | --- |
| 03 | What it means: Systems have two types of loops: reinforcing (snowball — growth feeds more growth) and balancing (self-correcting). Great learners design reinforcing loops deliberately. Applied to your voice training: Your daily recording → review → adjust cycle IS a feedback loop. Recording yourself is the data. Listening back is the feedback. Adjusting tomorrow's practice is the correction. Done daily, this becomes a reinforcing loop: better delivery → more confidence → better delivery. |
| 03 | Use this in: Every single day. This is the engine of your entire 30-day plan. |

**04  Second-Order Thinking    **Ask 'and then what?' after every decision

| 04 | Second-Order Thinking  See consequences beyond the obvious |
| --- | --- |
| 04 | What it means: Most people think one step ahead. Second-order thinking means asking 'and then what?' repeatedly after every decision to anticipate downstream effects — good and bad. Applied to your voice training: First-order: 'I'll practise breathing every day.' Second-order: 'My breath control improves, so I stop rushing sentences, so my audience can follow me, so I get more confident, so I take on bigger speaking opportunities.' Understanding this chain makes you more motivated to stay consistent even on hard days. |
| 04 | Use this in: Week 2 onwards — especially when motivation dips. Remind yourself of the chain. |

**05  Occam's Razor    **The simplest explanation is usually right

| 05 | Occam's Razor  Choose simplicity over complexity |
| --- | --- |
| 05 | What it means: When multiple explanations exist, the simplest one that fits the facts is most likely correct. Resist the urge to over-engineer solutions. Applied to your voice training: When your delivery feels off, don't overthink it. Ask: what is the simplest thing wrong right now? Usually it is one of three things — you are rushing, you are not breathing properly, or you are not pausing enough. Fix the simplest thing first before adding complexity. |
| 05 | Use this in: Week 3 — delivery refinement. Use when diagnosing problems in your recordings. |

**06  The Map is Not the Territory    **Your perception is never the full reality

| 06 | The Map Is Not the Territory  Question your self-perception |
| --- | --- |
| 06 | What it means: Your mental model of a situation is never a perfect copy of reality. The map (your belief) is not the territory (what is actually true). Always remain open to updating your beliefs with new data. Applied to your voice training: You may believe you sound nervous, too fast, or boring — but your recordings tell a different story. Many speakers are surprised that they sound more confident on playback than they felt in the moment. Let the recording be the territory; your feelings are just the map. Adjust accordingly. |
| 06 | Use this in: Week 1-2 — when reviewing your first recordings. Challenge your self-judgement with evidence. |

**07  The 10/10/10 Rule    **Use time perspective to make better decisions

| 07 | The 10/10/10 Rule  How will I feel about this in the future? |
| --- | --- |
| 07 | What it means: Before making a decision, ask: How will I feel about this in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years? This helps you override short-term discomfort to make long-term-aligned choices. Applied to your voice training: Skipping practice feels fine in 10 minutes. In 10 months, you are still the same speaker. In 10 years, you wish you had started. Attending a Toastmasters meeting feels terrifying in 10 minutes. In 10 months, you are unrecognisable. Use this model every time resistance shows up. |
| 07 | Use this in: Any moment you consider skipping practice or avoiding a live speaking opportunity. |

**08  Regret Minimisation    **Make decisions from your future self

| 08 | Regret Minimisation  Jeff Bezos built Amazon with this model |
| --- | --- |
| 08 | What it means: Imagine yourself at 80 years old looking back. Ask: which decision would I regret more — acting or not acting? This cuts through fear and short-term thinking powerfully. Applied to your voice training: At 80, will you regret: practising your voice for 25 minutes a day for 30 days? Almost certainly not. Will you regret never developing your communication skills and letting opportunities pass because you were not confident speaking? Very likely. This is your long game. |
| 08 | Use this in: Week 4 — performance phase. Use when fear of live speaking holds you back. |

# **How to Use These Models Daily**

## **Morning (Before Practice)**
Ask: What is the ONE thing I am working on today? (First Principles)
Ask: What bad habit am I specifically avoiding today? (Inversion)

## **During Practice**
Notice when something feels off — apply Occam's Razor to diagnose simply
If you catch yourself judging your voice — remember: the recording is the territory, not your feelings

## **After Practice (Review)**
Review your recording with fresh eyes — what does the data actually show?
Update tomorrow's focus based on what you heard (Feedback Loop)
Note one improvement, however small — feed the reinforcing loop

## **When Resistance Hits**
Don't want to practise? Apply 10/10/10 Rule
Afraid of a live speaking moment? Apply Regret Minimisation
Feeling overwhelmed? Apply Occam's Razor — just fix the simplest thing

**The speaker you want to become already exists.**
*These models are the thinking tools to close the gap.*
