# Narrative Case Study

**Example to match:** `ai-usecases/Case_01_Meridian_Fraud_Investigation_Agents.pdf`

## When to use
A dramatized, single-protagonist account of one specific technical/
governance challenge, told as a "transcript-style account" — this is a
different type from the engagement case study (see
references/engagement-case-study.md): narrower scope, one throughline
problem rather than a full engagement lifecycle, and told through a named
character's perspective rather than documentary sections.

## Additional frontmatter
```yaml
protagonist_role: ""     # e.g. "Enterprise AI Architect"
core_tension: ""          # one sentence naming the central problem, e.g. "holding the line on human decision authority under a 12-month Model Risk build"
```

## Required section skeleton
1. Title naming the throughline in one memorable line (match the pattern:
   "The Agent That Proposes, Never Disposes").
2. Subtitle stating the concrete system being built and the organization
   type (not the real company).
3. One-paragraph framing: protagonist, their role, the specific stakes.
4. "Cast of Characters" — every named participant with role + one-line
   background, so the reader can track who's who without re-introduction.
5. The narrative itself, following the protagonist through the build —
   structured as scenes/chapters at natural decision points, not a flat
   chronological log.

## Depth-of-research rubric
- Company and all named individuals are fictional — same rule as engagement
  case studies, never attach this to a real organization.
- The "core tension" must stay genuinely contested through most of the
  narrative — if the protagonist's position is obviously correct from
  paragraph one, it's not doing the job this format exists for (showing how
  a skilled practitioner navigates real disagreement, not just being right).
- Cast should be 5-8 named characters — enough for organizational realism
  (sponsor, skeptic, technical peer, compliance/risk voice, at minimum),
  not so many the reader loses track.
- This format only earns its place over a shorter guide/reference page when
  the value is genuinely in *how a decision got made*, not just *what the
  decision was* — if the content is really just "here's the correct
  architecture," write a guide instead.

## Formatting notes
- Keep the documentary/technical precision of the engagement-case-study
  type even inside the narrative — specific numbers, real architectural
  terms, actual failure modes — dramatization is in the framing, not a
  license to be vague about the technical content.
